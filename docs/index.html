I Kissed A Girl & I Liked It

A story about hearing yourself represented in music for the first time and chasing that high ever since

By Jan Diehm

with data work by Kelsey Campbell
and illustrations by Lace Garcia

In a time when the soundtrack to your life is built on a mountain of “firsts:”

💿 First CD: TLC’s “Creep.”

🤵🏻 First prom: Usher’s “Yeah!"

💔 First heartbreak: Eric Clapton’s “Wonderful Tonight.”

Those 8 problematically-presented words at the top of this piece became a secret anthem — the first time I heard a woman openly singing about another woman.

The first radio station to play “I Kissed a Girl” was Nashville’s 107.5 “The River,” or stereo preset No. 6 in my very inconspicuous first car: a lime green Pontiac Sunfire. When I heard it, I was floored. This? In the “Buckle of the Bible Belt” in a state that, to this day, has some of the most vile anti- queer and transgender laws? I immediately downloaded it and burned a CD.

At 3 minutes long, I could listen to the defiant bass line about 16 times on the 50-minute drive it took to get to the “second-closest” Best Buy two towns over. Driving those extra minutes and that extra town ensured that I could snag the equally inconspicuous flaming pink-boxed first season of L Word without waving a giant rainbow flag and raising any questions about my sexuality. I wasn’t ready. At least not yet.

But, it turned out that “I Kissed a Girl” wasn’t just something I was singing with my car windows rolled up, it was something everyone was scream-singing at the top of their lungs. It was a certified top-of-the-charts bop. I wasn’t fully convinced of mainstream acceptance, but it at least seemed like mainstream permission.

For as groundbreaking as it felt to hear a woman explicitly singing about being with another woman then, it would take another 13 years for a man explicitly singing about being with another man to top the charts.

Then came Lil Nas X’s “MONTERO (Call Me By Your Name).”

This time there’s no drunken permission: 🎵“I got so brave, drink in hand”🎵. No hint at a phase or a fling: 🎵“You're my experimental game”🎵. No fetishisation of the male gaze: 🎵“I hope my boyfriend don't mind it”🎵.

Just a Black queer artist unapolgetically owning it: 🎵“I want that jet lag from fuckin' and flyin', Shoot a child in your mouth while I'm ridin'”🎵

It’s going to be a soundtrack “first” for so many people. And it made me wonder: how many songs about same-gender relationships make it to the mainstream? So, I went there.

Since Katy Perry’s 2008 “gay-for-pay” bop, we found 7 other songs that appeared in the year-end Billboard Hot 100 with explicit same-gender lyrics where a man is singing about another man or a woman is singing about another woman.

Lady Gaga’s 2009 bisexual anthem “Poker Face” where she coins the phrase “bluffin' with my muffin” and sings about being with a man, but fantisizing about being with a woman:

🎵No, he can't read my poker face,
(She's got me like nobody)🎵

Britney Spears’ 2009 wordplay hit “If You Seek Amy” (say it slowly) where she sings:

🎵All of the boys and all of the girls are begging to, if you seek Amy🎵

Macklemore & Ryan Lewis’s 2013 hit “Same Love”, which features lesbian songwriter Mary Lambert singing the title hook from her own song “She Keeps Me Warm:”

🎵My love, my love, my love,
She keeps me warm.🎵

Little Big Town’s 2015 tease “Girl Crush” which the band says is about a woman obsessing over another woman because that woman stole her man. It still caused a stir in the country music industry with lyrics like:

🎵 I wanna taste her lips, yeah,
'cause they taste like you 🎵

Halsey’s 2018 hit “Bad at Love” where she switches back and forth between masculine and feminine pronouns for the verses. The second verse opens:

🎵 Got a girl with California eyes,
And I thought that she could really be the one this time 🎵

Cardi B’s 2018 “Bartier Cardi” where the openly bisexual rapper throws out the line:

🎵I got your bitch and she naked,
Ice on the cake when I bake it🎵

And Cardi B’s 2019 single “Money” where she talks about leaving an Atlanta strip club with a woman:

🎵Walk out Follie's with a bitch,
Bring a thottie to the whip🎵

That’s 0.7%. Eight songs, not even one a year, out of 1,157 unique hits (some songs on the year-end Billboard Hot 100 appear across multiple years). And ALL of them feature a woman explicitly singing about another woman using words like “she”, “her”, and “girl” — until now.

I’m making some assumptions to say that “MONTERO (Call Me By Your Name)” will appear on the 2021 year-end Billboard Hot 100 charts, but it’s well within reach. Lil Nas X’s queer anthem has already spent 9 weeks on the charts. The No. 100 songs from the last 3 years average about 20 weeks in the Hot 100.

Out queer artists like Janelle Monáe, Frank Ocean, Kelani, Hayley Kiyoko, Troye Sivan, and JoJo Siwa haven’t cracked the year-end Billboard Hot 100 with solo songs yet, but even when queer artists do reach the pinacle of pop mainstream they rarely sing about same-gender relationships.

Of course, queer lyrics aren’t limited to same-gender references, but they’re often the most sanitized and direct version that a mainstream audience recognizes and is exposed to.

Between 2008–2020, we identifed 146 songs featuring an openly queer artist in the year-end Billboard Top 100. Of those, we found 4 songs where explicit same-gender lyrics were used in tandem with opposite-gender lyrics.

Artists like Lady Gaga, Halsey, and Cardi B are openly bisexual so singing about relationships with BOTH men and women is queer. And artists like Demi Lovato, Sam Smith, and Miley Cyrus are non-binary or genderfluid, so when they do sing about relationships it’s queer. And there are plenty of other queer lyrics that don’t explicitly reference gender at all.

As someone firmly planted in my queer identity now (I have a wife, we have a rainbow flag in front of our home, I watch a lot of women’s sports) it’s easy for me to catch enuedos, nods, and winks like Lovato’s 🎵“Got a taste for the cherry, I just need to take a bite”🎵 or Lil Nas X’s 🎵“I might bottom on the low, but I top shit,”🎵 but thinking back to who I was when I first heard “I Kissed a Girl,” I might not have recognized myself in these songs unless someone pointed a giant flashing neon rainbow arrow at it.

It reminds me of a line from a Dave Holmes piece about Taylor Swift’s the “vague call for tolerance” that was “You Need to Calm Down:” “...in a bedroom somewhere in the world, some young person is getting a desperately-needed window into a life that suits them, and I am legitimately thrilled for that kid.”

And that’s what music can do. It can be someone’s “hundredth” or “thousandth” or “not-far-enough,” AND it can be someone’s “first.”

Methods & Notes

We collected year-end Hot 100 songs from Billboard. Then we researched each artist’s sexual orientation and gender identity with a mixed programmatic/manual approach that looked at Wikipedia pages, news articles, and social media profiles. We built 3 dictionaries: one to flag feminine gendered lyrics like “women”, “girl”, “her”, and “she”; one to flag masculine gendered lyrics like “man”, “boy”, “his”, and “he”; and one to flag relationship lyrics like “love”, “heart”, “kiss”, “sex”.

We used lyrics from an existing corpus and filled in gaps from Genius. Each song was tagged by whether it contained feminine gendered lyrics, masculine gendered lyrics, and relationship lyrics. Then those tags were compared to the artist’s sexual orientation and gender identity. This gave us a set of songs to manually check. From there we listened to A LOT of music to identify the 8 songs with explicit same-gender relationship lyrics. Since this was an imperfect manual effort, we might have missed something. Got a song we should add? Email Jan at jan@pudding.cool.

I began to poke around at this story in 2019 and enlisted the help of GaytaScience’s Kelsey Cambell. Anecdotally, I felt like I was hearing more explicit same-gender references in mainstream songs than when I was a teenager, but after looking at the initial data, my music tastes might have just gotten a little more queer.

I held onto this story because it still felt like it was worth telling, even if I didn’t think the data could be the main driver. I was taunted with songs like Sam Smith’s and Demi Lovato's “I’m Ready” and Miley Cyrus’ “Midnight Sky” until Lil Nas X released “MONTERO (Call Me By Your Name)” and I realized it wasn’t the absolute number of obviously queer songs that made the top charts, it was that there was a single song where I felt represented at all.

This story is about my unique queer journey and the music that shaped it. It is not meant to be representative of the full queer commmunity. I’ve chosen to use the word “queer” throughout this piece because it’s how I personally identify and to respect the intersections and inclusivity of the LGBTQIA+ community.
